# Gold vs Silver: A Risk-Return Analysis (2020–2025)

## Abstract
This project presents an exploratory analysis comparing the risk and return profiles of Gold and Silver futures using daily price data from January 2020 to December 2025. The study covers one of the most turbulent periods in recent financial history, including the COVID-19 crash, the post-pandemic inflation increase, among other relevant events.

The study includes daily and cumulative returns, volatility metrics, maximum drawdown, the Gold/Silver ratio and the correlation between both assets.

## Data
- **Source:** Investing.com
- **Assets:** Gold Futures (GC) and Silver Futures (SI)
- **Frequency:** Daily
- **Period:** January 2020 - December 2025
- **Tool:** Microsoft Excel
To consider: variations between the obtained data and other sources may differ due to future contracts expiration. 

## Methodology
Daily returns were calculated as the percentage change in closing prices between consecutive trading days. Cumulative returns were computed relative to the first observation (January 2020). Annualized volatility was obtained by multiplying daily volatility by the square root of 252 (standard trading days per year). Maximum drawdown was calculated as the largest peak-to-trough decline over the full period.

## Results

| Metric | Gold | Silver |
|---|---|---|
| Average Daily Return | 0.10% | 0.14% |
| Daily Volatility | 1.21% | 2.43% |
| Annualized Volatility | 19.2% | 38.6% |
| Total Cumulative Return | 187% | 291% |
| Maximum Drawdown | -21.5% | -40.1% |
| Returns Correlation | 0.92 | 0.92 |

## Key Findings

**Returns**
Silver outperformed Gold over the period, delivering a total cumulative return of 291% vs 187% for Gold. However, Silver's higher return came with significantly higher risk, consistent with the classic risk-return tradeoff.

**Volatility**
Silver showed considerably higher volatility than Gold, with an annualized volatility of 38.6% compared to 19.2% for Gold. Gold's lower volatility confirms its well-established role as a safe-haven asset and store of value.

**Drawdown**
Silver experienced a maximum drawdown of -40.1%, nearly double Gold's -21.5%. This highlights Silver's more speculative and cyclical nature relative to Gold, partly explained by its significant industrial demand component.

**Correlation**
Both assets showed a high positive correlation of 0.92, confirming they tend to move together driven by common macro factors such as real interest rates, inflation expectations and the US dollar. However, their different magnitudes of response reveal clearly distinct risk profiles.

**Gold/Silver Ratio**
The ratio peaked above 120 during the COVID-19 crash in March 2020, reflecting Gold's stronger safe-haven demand in crisis periods, a historically extreme level. It has since normalized toward levels around 70-80, consistent with long-term historical averages.

## Market Context

**2020 - COVID-19 Crash**
Both metals sold off sharply in March 2020 as investors liquidated assets for cash during the pandemic start. Gold recovered faster, pushing the Gold/Silver Ratio above 120, as mentioned. Silver then rebounded strongly as governments injected large amounts of money into the economy, raising fears of inflation.

**2021–2022 - Inflation and Rate Increases**
The post-COVID inflation surge initially benefited both metals. However, the sharp rise in interest rates by the Federal Reserve from 2022 created pressure on both metals, as higher rates make them less attractive compared to other investments.

**2024–2025 - Gold's Historic Rally**
Gold reached new all-time highs driven by central bank buying, geopolitical uncertainty and expectations of rate cuts. Silver followed with a delayed but stronger move, consistent with its historically more volatile behavior.

## Interpretation
The data confirms the classic relationship between the two metals: Gold acts as a stable store of value with lower volatility and drawdown, while Silver behaves more like an industrial commodity with higher return potential but greater risk. The high correlation suggests they share common macro drivers, but their risk-return profiles are clearly distinct, a relevant consideration for portfolio construction and asset allocation decisions.
